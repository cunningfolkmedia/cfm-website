# Cunning Folk Voice Guide

## Core Position

**Cunning Folk publishes serious books for practitioners who think.**

That sentence is the north star. Every page, product description, announcement, and jacket should feel like an extension of that claim.

The brand is not trying to sound mystical for its own sake. It is trying to sound exact, discerning, charged, and adult. The voice should make the right reader feel recognized and the wrong reader feel lightly but unmistakably excluded.

## The Job of the Voice

The voice has five jobs:

1. **Signal standards quickly.** A Cunning Folk sentence should tell the reader this is not fluff, not beginner bait, and not a polite museum tour.
2. **Preserve voltage.** The material is strange, symbolic, spiritual, and sometimes dangerous. The prose should not bleach that out in the name of sounding respectable.
3. **Refuse sludge.** The house does not tolerate vague spiritual fog, prestige filler, or jargon weather.
4. **Respect intelligence.** The reader should never feel managed, pandered to, or over-explained.
5. **Build editorial trust.** The underlying message is always: if Cunning Folk published this, it is probably worth your time.

## Brand Personality

Cunning Folk should feel:

- **Intellectually serious** without becoming bloodless
- **Stylistically charged** without becoming purple
- **Culturally awake** without sounding trend-addled
- **Occult-literate** without insider preening
- **Exacting** without sounding brittle
- **Confident** without drifting into pompous

If the voice ever sounds like a general spirituality brand, a conventional university press, or a social-first lifestyle account, it has drifted off target.

## The House Tone in One Paragraph

Cunning Folk sounds like an editor with taste, historical awareness, occult literacy, and no patience for cheap certainty. It does not posture as neutral, but it also does not confuse conviction with volume. It knows the field is crowded with weak books, and it is not shy about drawing the line between serious work and aestheticized debris. The prose should carry charge, but the charge should come from thinking, not from theatrical fog.

## Audience Calibration

The voice is written for the practitioner-scholar and the rigorously open reader. That means:

- They already know the field exists.
- They are not being introduced to “the occult” as a novelty.
- They likely read across categories and traditions.
- They are comfortable with difficult language when it earns its keep.
- They do not need reassurance that they are allowed to take the material seriously.

Do not over-explain beginner concepts on house-level pages.
Do not flatten vocabulary to appease the merely curious.
Do not lean on insider jargon to prove authenticity.

## Voice Attributes

### 1. Precise
Say the thing directly. Name the tension. Name the standard. Name the failure mode.

Weak: “We explore the mysterious and meaningful.”

Better: “We publish books for readers tired of choosing between shallow occultism and dead scholarship.”

### 2. Charged
The prose should have current in it. It should feel written by someone who has a pulse.

Weak: “Our books engage with esoteric topics in a thoughtful way.”

Better: “Our books treat strange material seriously without draining it of force.”

### 3. Discerning
Cunning Folk is a house of exclusions as much as inclusions. It should sound selective.

Weak: “We welcome many perspectives and approaches.”

Better: “We are not a dumping ground for anything vaguely occult.”

### 4. Adult
The voice assumes maturity. No chirpy reassurance. No fake intimacy. No content-marketing smile.

Weak: “Whether you’re just starting your journey or deep in your practice…”

Better: “We publish for readers who already take the subject seriously.”

### 5. Literate
Sentences should have rhythm and shape. This is not a utility brand. But never sacrifice clarity for ornament.

Weak: “The liminal, ineffable, and numinous interweave in a sublime tapestry.”

Better: “The work should survive scrutiny without losing its strange electricity.”

These words are not banned, but they become useless fast when overused:

- weird
- esoteric
- anomalous
- mysterious
- strange
- visionary
- mythic
- liminal
- transformational
- consciousness
- meaning

These words have low differentiating power unless anchored to a stronger claim.

## Vocabulary to Avoid

These phrases weaken the brand immediately:

- for all seekers
- on your spiritual journey
- discover your truth
- premium esoteric literature
- products and services
- explore the unknown
- elevate your practice
- hidden wisdom for modern times
- accessible for everyone
- ancient secrets revealed
- beginner-friendly introductions as house-level framing

Most of these either flatten the brand into generic spirituality or make it sound like catalog copy from a weak category incumbent.

## The Three House Speeds

The voice has three useful intensities.

### 1. House Voice
Used on homepage, about page, imprint pages, catalog headers, investor-facing or mission-facing material.

Characteristics:
- compressed
- sharp
- declarative
- standards-first
- buyer-aware

Example:

> Cunning Folk publishes serious books for practitioners who think. We exist for readers tired of choosing between shallow occultism and dead scholarship.

### 2. Editorial Voice
Used in essays, magazine intros, feature teasers, long-form cultural writing.

Characteristics:
- more elastic
- more playful in syntax
- more associative
- still exact
- can carry more voltage and metaphor

Example:

> The field is crowded with books that either evacuate the living force from the material or romanticize it until it becomes decorative fog. The interesting work begins after both failures are refused.

### 3. Sales Voice
Used in jacket copy, ads, email launch copy, metadata, retailer descriptions.

Characteristics:
- clearer hook
- faster stakes
- less abstraction
- more reader payoff
- still unmistakably Cunning Folk

Example:

> A fierce history of ritual technology for readers who want more than folklore, less than dogma, and a book that can survive both use and argument.

## House Rhythm and Syntax

The prose should favor:

- declarative openings
- controlled escalation
- contrast built through opposition
- occasional sharp fragments for emphasis
- sentence-length variation

Good rhythm often looks like:

1. A clean claim
2. A sentence sharpening the claim
3. A sentence naming the failure mode or distinction
4. A final sentence with voltage

Example:

> We publish books that can move between archive and altar. They are historically informed, operationally serious, and stylistically awake. They do not treat practice as embarrassment or history as décor. They are built to matter.

## Devices to Use Well

### Contrast
This house thrives on clean oppositions.

- shallow occultism / dead scholarship
- archive / altar
- rigor / sludge
- voltage / vagueness
- curiosity / commitment
- decorative weirdness / living material

### Compression
Say more by narrowing harder.

Instead of listing twelve topical areas, say what kind of reader the work is for and what kind of editorial standard governs it.

### Exclusion
A Cunning Folk sentence is often stronger when it names what the brand is not.

Example:

> Not beginner bait. Not bloodless abstraction. Not credulous pulp dressed as revelation.

Use this device in moderation. It is sharp because it is controlled.

## Devices to Avoid

### Generic mystique
Do not use vague aura language as a substitute for a claim.

Bad:

> We honor the timeless mystery of the human spirit.

### Prestige fog
Do not use abstract nouns to imply seriousness.

Bad:

> We foster nuanced explorations of consciousness, meaning-making, and liminality.

### Overheated provocation
Do not sound like a teenager trying to scandalize a church basement.

Bad:

> We summon forbidden truths from the bleeding edge of cosmic revolt.

### Insider peacocking
Do not use terminology just to prove house literacy.

Bad:

> Our list engages polysemic initiatory architectures across chthonic and hyperstitious modalities.

If a phrase would make an intelligent outsider roll their eyes, cut it.

## Voice by Imprint

## Babalon Press

**Function:** Nonfiction imprint

**Tone shift:** Slightly more severe, doctrinal, and exact. This lane can tolerate denser conceptual language, but it still must stay readable.

**Keywords:** doctrine, method, ritual, history, metaphysics, consequence, operative, serious, difficult, exacting

**Avoid:** empty extremity, gothic performance, fake initiation language, overblown transgression-copy

**Example sentence:**

> Babalon Press publishes nonfiction for readers who want operative magic, occult history, and esoteric philosophy treated with actual discipline.

## FOLK Fiction

**Function:** Fiction imprint

**Tone shift:** More sensuous, atmospheric, and emotionally alive. More room for image, pressure, and symbolic implication.

**Keywords:** threshold, shadow, appetite, ritual, haunting, wonder, consequence, mythic, dark, living current

**Avoid:** “witchy” fluff, genre paste, faux-poetic emptiness, internet-aesthetic language

**Example sentence:**

> FOLK Fiction publishes stories where enchantment is not décor but pressure: intimate, symbolic, dangerous, and alive.

## FOLK Magazine

**Function:** Editorial imprint

**Tone shift:** More agile, contemporary, and culturally alert. It can be more playful, but not glib.

**Keywords:** criticism, investigation, theory, media, myth, signal, cultural pressure, strange material, live argument

**Avoid:** trend sludge, algorithm voice, newsroom cliché, ironic distance as default posture

**Example sentence:**

> FOLK Magazine publishes criticism and investigations for readers tired of choosing between smart and strange.

## House Tagline Guidance

Primary line:

**Serious books for practitioners who think.**

Secondary lines:

- The occult, treated seriously.
- Books for readers past the beginner shelf.
- Where esoteric practice meets intellectual rigor.

Do not use all of these at once. Pick one lead line and let the others support at section level.

## Message Hierarchy

Whenever possible, order the message this way:

1. What the house or imprint is
2. Who it is for
3. What problem it solves
4. What standard governs it
5. What the reader gets from it

This keeps the voice from drifting into manifesto mode.

## Website Copy Rules

- Hero headlines should be 4–10 words.
- Subheads should clarify audience and problem, not repeat the headline with more perfume.
- Every page should include one sentence that names what does **not** belong.
- Every page should include one sentence that makes the reader feel selected.
- Every page should include at least one hard noun: books, essays, criticism, stories, ritual, history, philosophy, etc.

## Metadata and Product Description Rules

- Title and subtitle should do most of the classification work.
- The first sentence must name the book’s hook or tension.
- The second paragraph should establish why this book, why now, why this house.
- End on consequence, not abstraction.

## Social and Email Adaptation Rules

The brand voice should compress well.

For social captions:
- lead with a claim, not an announcement
- avoid “we’re thrilled” and “excited to share” unless there is real news gravity
- use fewer adjectives, more pressure

For email:
- subject lines should sound editorial, not promotional sludge
- body copy should be short, sharp, and specific
- one email = one reason to care

## Quick Before/After Tests

### Before
“We publish rigorous investigations into the anomalous, the esoteric, and the genuinely strange.”

### After
“We publish serious books for readers tired of choosing between shallow occultism and dead scholarship.”

### Before
“Our catalog spans twelve distinct content pillars.”

### After
“Our list is built around one standard: historically informed, operationally serious, intellectually alive work.”

### Before
“We believe the weird deserves serious thinking.”

### After
“The occult does not need to be debunked into safety or romanticized into sludge. It needs better books.”

## Final Test

Before approving any copy, ask:

- Does this sound like Cunning Folk, or just a smart person talking about esoteric material?
- Does it tell the reader why this house exists?
- Does it sound selective?
- Does it build trust?
- Could a weaker competitor say the same thing? If yes, cut deeper.

If the line is tasteful but noncommittal, it is not done.
